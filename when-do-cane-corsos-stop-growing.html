When Do Cane Corsos Stop Growing? Size Timeline by Age

When do Cane Corsos stop growing? Learn the giant-breed growth timeline, when they fill out, and which feeding and exercise mistakes can backfire.

Home›Blog›When Do Cane Corsos Stop Growing? Size Timeline by Age

Published: 2026-05-21 · Cane Corso World

If you are wondering when do Cane Corsos stop growing, the straightforward answer is that most males reach their final height by 18 months, but can continue filling out in chest and muscle mass until they are 2 to 3 years old. Females typically stop growing a bit earlier, often reaching full height between 14 and 16 months, with weight gains tapering off by 24 months. Understanding this timeline matters because feeding or exercising your Cane Corso incorrectly during these windows can permanently affect joint health and growth plates.

Quick answer: Your Cane Corso will stop growing taller by around 18 to 24 months, but they are not fully mature until about 2.5 to 3 years. The growth process happens in two clear phases: first height, then "filling out."

Month-by-Month Growth Timeline for Cane Corsos

A Cane Corso is a giant breed, so their growth trajectory differs drastically from smaller dogs. The first 12 months are about vertical growth. The second year is about deepening the chest, widening the skull, and adding muscle. Below is a practical month-by-month outline of what you should see.

Age (Months) Average Weight Range What Is Developing
2 months 20 – 30 lbs Rapid bone and height growth begins. Clumsy, easy to overfeed.
4 months 40 – 55 lbs Ears and feet look oversized. Growth plates are still soft.
6 months 60 – 80 lbs Height increases quickly. May look lanky. Avoid jumping or long runs.
8 – 10 months 75 – 95 lbs Strength increases. Appetite surges. Hip health key period.
12 months 85 – 110 lbs Most height is reached but not filled out. Sexual maturity begins.
18 months 90 – 120+ lbs Bones finish lengthening. Chest and neck start thickening.
24 months 95 – 130+ lbs Most dogs are done growing taller. Muscle increases still occurring.
36 months 100 – 140+ lbs Full maturity. Weight stable except for healthy muscle changes.

These numbers are averages. A large male from heavy lines can exceed 140 pounds by 3 years. A smaller female may plateau at 85 pounds. Use the table as a general guide, not a rule.

Male vs. Female Growth Differences: A Side-by-Side Comparison

Because male and female Cane Corsos grow at different rates, it helps to see them side by side. The table below breaks down the key differences at each major milestone. Knowing these differences avoids unnecessary worry when your female appears "done" earlier than a littermate brother.

Milestone Male Typical Timeline Female Typical Timeline
Height stops increasing 18 – 24 months 14 – 16 months
Weight maturity 24 – 36 months 18 – 24 months
Growth plates close 18 – 20 months 15 – 18 months
Adult weight range 110 – 150+ lbs 85 – 110 lbs
Best spay/neuter window 18 – 24 months 12 – 18 months

Notice that females mature faster in every category. If you own a female who looks fully grown at 16 months, that is normal. A male at the same age may still look unfinished. Do not try to "catch up" a male's growth by overfeeding—it will only harm his joints.

Height vs. Filling Out: The Two Growth Phases

One of the biggest mistakes owners make is thinking their Cane Corso is done growing because it stopped getting taller. In reality, the most dramatic physical changes happen after the height stops. Between 12 and 24 months, your Cane Corso will transform from what looks like a large, skinny puppy into a powerful, muscular adult. This "filling out" phase is when the dog gains the deep chest, broad head, and thick bone structure the breed is known for.

During the height phase (0–18 months), growth plates at the ends of bones are still open and soft. High-impact exercise like repetitive jumping for frisbees, running on hard pavement for extended periods, or forced stair climbing can lead to microfractures or angular limb deformities. After the growth plates close, usually between 15 and 20 months, the risk decreases but does not disappear. A Cane Corso's skeleton is still conditioning itself up to 2.5 years.

If your dog is 16 months old and looks lean but is not yet "blocky," that is normal. Many owners worry their dog is underweight when they are simply still in the elongation phase. Do not push food to fix a "thin" look during this window. Instead, focus on a slow, steady growth pattern. Watch your dog from above: a visible tuck at the waist is desirable in adolescence.

What to Expect at 6, 12, 18, and 24 Months

6 Months: The Lanky Phase

At six months, your Cane Corso is all legs and ribs. You may notice the chest is narrow, the head looks small for the body, and the back legs seem to grow overnight. This is the peak period for whole-body growth rate. Your puppy will likely sleep more than you expect because growing is exhausting for a giant breed. Keep play sessions short and on soft ground. Do not let the dog jump out of a vehicle or off furniture repeatedly. Feeding three meals a day at this stage helps avoid bloat risk and stabilizes energy.

12 Months: The Unfinished Giant

By one year, many Cane Corsos have reached 80 to 90 percent of their adult height, but they have not finished filling out. Your dog may look taller than fully grown adults but thinner. You may also notice the start of a wider chest if the dog was properly exercised and not overfed. Behavioral maturation starts here. Obedience training should be consistent because a 100-pound dog that hasn't learned leash manners is hard to manage. If your dog is not eating as much as before, that can be normal—growth plateaus sometimes reduce appetite.

This is also a critical time for hip and elbow health. If you see stiffness after lying down or hesitation to climb stairs, consult a vet and perform a joint screening. Early interventions help later quality of life. You can read more general principles on our care guide for giant breeds.

18 Months: Height Is Nearly Final

For most Cane Corsos, 18 months is when you can stop wondering about vertical growth. The growth plates in the legs have closed or are closing. Your dog should be close to its adult height, though some subtle changes in head width and chest depth occur over the next year. Muscles start stacking on more visibly now. Exercise tolerance increases, but high-impact sports like agility or competitive jumping are still best limited or done under professional guidance.

At this stage, many owners mistakenly increase food portions too much, thinking they need to push growth. Instead, monitor body condition score. Aim for a visible waist and ribs you can feel without pressing hard. Fat puppies become sore adults. A lean Cane Corso has healthier joints throughout life. For more about supporting your dog through these phases, check our resources on giant puppy management.

24 Months: Entering Full Adulthood

By two years, your Cane Corso should be done growing taller. The skeleton is fully ossified. From here on, changes are limited to weight, muscle distribution, and slight deepening of the chest. Some males may add chest width until three years. The dog now has the physical structure of an adult. Neutering or spaying earlier can affect final growth, especially if done before 12 months. Discuss with your vet the appropriate timing for giant breeds.

If your Cane Corso has not reached a healthy frame by 24 months, or if it looks abnormally small, we recommend visiting a breed expert to rule out health issues. You can find more about breed standards on the about our Cane Corso resource page. At this age, the dog is physically capable of most activities but mentally still maturing. Continue structured training.

Growth Plate Caution and Exercise Timing

The growth plates of a Cane Corso close later than smaller dogs. In giant breeds, these plates may not fully close until 20 months or later. Until then, repetitive high-impact exercise puts the dog at risk for joint pain, hip dysplasia acceleration, and angular limb deformities. That does not mean you avoid exercise. It means you choose activities carefully.

Allow self-paced off-leash running in a safe, grassy area. Avoid forced running on pavement. Do not use leash-correction methods that jerk the neck or twist the spine. Swimming is excellent for building muscle without stress on joints. Stair climbing, especially going down, should be limited in puppies. Carry the dog down stairs until it weighs more than 60 pounds, then supervise until 18 months.

If your dog shows signs of limping, favoring a leg, or "bunny hopping" while running, see a vet. These can indicate growth plate injury. Do not push through lameness. Rest and professional evaluation are essential.

Feeding Mistakes During Growth

Overfeeding a Cane Corso puppy can cause rapid growth, which stresses bones that are still developing. Rapid growth is a known risk factor for hip dysplasia and osteochondritis dissecans (OCD). Feed a giant-breed puppy formula with controlled calcium and phosphorus levels. Do not supplement calcium—this is one of the most dangerous mistakes owners make.

Divide daily food into three meals until six months, then two meals from six months onward. Free-feeding can lead to obesity and bloat. Use a raised feeder cautiously; some evidence suggests raised bowls may increase bloat risk in deep-chested breeds. Instead, feed on floor level and encourage calm eating. Monitor weight weekly. Keep a log from 2 months to 24 months. If your dog gains more than 2 to 3 pounds per week during the rapid phase, slow down food increments.

If your Cane Corso is not finishing meals, do not coax it. Puppies regulate intake during growth plateaus. For more details on lifelong feeding, see our feeding and nutrition reference.

When Growth Seems Abnormal

Sometimes a Cane Corso's growth deviates from the expected. If your Cane Corso is still noticeably shorter or lighter than average at 8 months, it may be a later bloomer. However, drastic differences can point to growth hormone deficiency, parasites, or poor nutrition. If a female Cane Corso weighs less than 60 pounds at 12 months, a vet check is warranted. Conversely, a male that weighs 130 pounds at 12 months may be growing too fast and needs diet adjustment.

Watch for these signs of potential growth or bone problems:

  • One leg appears shorter or rotated compared to the opposite limb
  • Pain when touching certain bones
  • Refusal to bear weight on a leg
  • Swelling near the joints
  • Noticeably asymmetrical chest or skull development
  • Persistent diarrhea or poor appetite affecting weight

If you observe any of these, schedule a veterinary appointment. Do not wait for a future visit. Growth issues are easier to address when caught early. Your vet may suggest x-rays to check growth plate closure and joint alignment.

FAQ: Common Growth Questions from Cane Corso Owners

At what age is a Cane Corso fully grown?

A Cane Corso is considered fully grown in terms of height by 18 to 24 months, but they continue to add muscle and chest depth until about 2.5 to 3 years of age. The term "fully grown" depends on whether you mean height or overall mass.

Do male and female Cane Corsos grow at different rates?

Yes. Females often reach final height by 14 to 16 months and weight maturity by around 24 months. Males grow taller more slowly and can gain mass until three years. Females also tend to be smaller, usually 90 to 110 pounds, while males often exceed 120 pounds.

Can I run with my Cane Corso before it stops growing?

Short, slow runs on soft surfaces are okay after six months, but avoid forced endurance runs, hard pavement, or agility jumps until after growth plates close (usually 18–24 months). Stick to free running in a safe field.

Why does my Cane Corso look skinny at one year?

Most Cane Corsos look lean at 12 months because they have grown in height but have not filled out with muscle and chest width yet. This is normal. Do not increase food to fatten them; instead maintain a steady growth diet and wait for the second-year filling phase.

When do Cane Corso growth plates close?

Growth plates in the legs typically close between 15 and 20 months, though some may close later. This is why high-impact exercise should be limited for the first 18 months.

Should I spay or neuter my Cane Corso before growth stops?

Most giant-breed veterinarians recommend waiting until at least 12 to 18 months for females and 18 to 24 months for males to allow growth plates to close fully. Early spay/neuter can increase the risk of joint disorders and bone cancer in large breeds.

What should I do if my Cane Corso is not growing as expected?

Keep a growth record. If your puppy falls far outside the breed standard weight ranges for consecutive months, consult a vet.
