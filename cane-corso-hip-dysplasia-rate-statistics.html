Cane Corso Hip Dysplasia Rate Statistics by Age and Screening Stage

Cane Corso hip dysplasia rate statistics with age-based risk context, OFA screening timing, and prevention priorities.

Published: 2026-05-21 · Cane Corso World

The Cane Corso is a majestic and powerful breed, but its large size and rapid growth come with significant orthopedic risks. Among them, hip dysplasia stands out as the most common developmental joint disease, affecting quality of life and mobility. Understanding Cane Corso hip dysplasia rate statistics is critical for owners who want to make informed decisions about screening, nutrition, and early intervention. This article provides a data-driven breakdown of prevalence rates by age, the role of OFA screening timing, and practical prevention priorities—all sourced from veterinary research and breed health registries.

Hip dysplasia is a multifactorial condition where genetics, growth rate, and environmental factors converge. For a giant breed like the Cane Corso, the stakes are high, as untreated dysplasia can lead to debilitating osteoarthritis within just a few years. Published research on Cane Corso hip dysplasia confirms that the condition is both hereditary and polygenic, meaning responsible breeding and early screening are essential for reducing prevalence. Understanding the statistics is the first step for any owner committed to their dog's long-term health. What Is Hip Dysplasia and Why Should Cane Corso Owners Care?

Hip dysplasia is a malformation of the coxofemoral joint where the femoral head does not fit snugly into the acetabulum. This laxity leads to abnormal wear, inflammation, osteoarthritis, and pain. For a giant breed like the Cane Corso, the consequences are severe: lameness, reluctance to exercise, and long-term arthritis can develop as early as 12 to 18 months of age. Published research on Cane Corso hip dysplasia confirms that the condition is hereditary and polygenic, meaning responsible breeding and early screening are essential.

Without screening, many dogs remain undiagnosed until clinical signs appear. The Orthopedic Foundation for Animals (OFA) provides the gold standard for diagnosis, and their hip dysplasia database allows owners and breeders to check individual scores. OFA ratings range from Excellent to Dysplastic, and any dog with a rating of Fair or below is considered to have some degree of hip dysplasia. This is why understanding the specific rate statistics for the Cane Corso is vital for preventive care. Many owners also find our Cane Corso weight chart useful for monitoring growth.

Cane Corso Hip Dysplasia Rate Statistics: The Big Picture

To understand how widespread hip dysplasia is among Cane Corso, owners must rely on breed-specific data rather than general large-breed averages. According to the OFA statistics for Cane Corso (as of the most recent data), the breed has a reported hip dysplasia rate of approximately 19.8%. This means roughly 1 in 5 Cane Corso dogs that have been screened and submitted to the OFA database receive a dysplastic rating.

However, this number should be interpreted with caution because the OFA dataset is voluntary and may overrepresent dogs from responsible breeders who screen regularly. The true prevalence in the wider population could be higher, especially among dogs from unregulated sources. A 2020 review of canine hip dysplasia in giant breeds noted that prevalence rates in untested populations can be double those reported by registries. This makes owner education and proactive screening all the more important.

Age Statistics: When Does Hip Dysplasia First Appear?

Early-Onset Dysplasia: 4 to 12 Months

In rapidly growing Cane Corso puppies, early signs of hip dysplasia can be seen as early as 4 months of age. Veterinary studies on giant breed hip development indicate that excessive joint laxity at 4 to 6 months strongly predicts future osteoarthritis. Puppies that show a "bunny-hopping" gait, difficulty rising, or reluctance to go up stairs should be evaluated immediately. Early detection via PennHIP or OFA preliminary evaluation at 12 months can catch dysplasia before severe arthritis sets in. Owners should also be aware that teething discomfort can sometimes mask early orthopedic issues.

Adolescence and Young Adulthood: 1 to 3 Years

For many Cane Corso, clinical signs of hip dysplasia become most apparent between 12 and 36 months. This is when the dog reaches full skeletal maturity—typically around 18 to 24 months, as outlined in our guide on when Cane Corso stop growing. During this period, the joint may become unstable under the weight of a fully grown dog (90 to 110 pounds for females, 110 to 130 pounds for males). The OFA recommends final certification at 24 months, but preliminary scores can be obtained earlier. Stiffness after exercise is a common early sign at this stage. Ensure your dog's exercise routine is appropriate by reading about Cane Corso exercise needs.

Adulthood and Osteoarthritis: 4 Years and Older

As the dog ages, undiagnosed hip dysplasia inevitably progresses to osteoarthritis. A longitudinal study from the veterinary literature on giant breeds found that by 6 years of age, 80% of dogs with Fair or Dysplastic OFA scores had radiographic evidence of osteoarthritis. For Cane Corso owners, this means that a dog with mild symptoms at 2 years may become significantly lame by 6 to 8 years. Chronic management becomes crucial at this stage. For tips on senior care, see our Cane Corso lifespan guide.

OFA Screening Stages and Timing for Cane Corso

PennHIP: The Earlier and More Sensitive Test

PennHIP (Pennsylvania Hip Improvement Program) can be performed as early as 16 weeks of age and provides a distraction index (DI) that measures passive joint laxity. DI values above 0.30 in giant breeds are strongly correlated with future osteoarthritis. Research on PennHIP in giant breeds shows it is more predictive than OFA screening for early-life owner decisions, such as modifying growth plans. This early data can be invaluable for proactive owners. Understanding Cane Corso temperament can also help you interpret changes in your dog's behavior related to pain.

OFA Preliminary: 12 to 24 Months

The OFA offers a preliminary evaluation for dogs 12 to 23 months of age. While not a final score, it provides valuable insight. Approximately 12% of Cane Corso receiving a Preliminary Fair rating later improve to Good or Excellent after full maturity, but 88% remain the same or worsen. Owners should not ignore a Fair preliminary rating. It is a strong indicator that the dog's hips are not developing ideally and require careful management.

OFA Final: 24 Months

At 24 months, the OFA issues a final certification. This is the accepted breed standard for breeding decisions. The AKC breed standard does not require OFA certification, but responsible breeders voluntarily submit radiographs. The rate of Fair or Dysplastic final certifications in Cane Corso—nearly 50%—underscores why buyers should always ask for OFA scores on both parents.

Risk Factors That Increase the Cane Corso Hip Dysplasia Rate

Genetics and Lineage

The heritability of hip dysplasia in Cane Corso is estimated at 0.35 to 0.45 based on breed-specific genetic studies. This means that if one parent has dysplasia, the offspring have a dramatically elevated risk. A puppy from two OFA Excellent parents has less than a 10% chance of dysplasia, while a puppy from two Fair or Dysplastic parents has a risk exceeding 50%. Lineage is the most critical risk factor. When evaluating a breeder, ask not only about the parents but also about grandparents. For more on selecting a breeder, read our guide to buying a Cane Corso puppy.

Excess Growth Rate and Weight

Rapid growth is a well-documented environmental risk factor. Giant breed studies have shown that dogs fed ad libitum (free feeding) compared to portion-controlled diets have significantly higher rates of hip laxity. Overweight puppies at 6 months of age have a 2.5 times higher risk of developing clinical hip dysplasia. For Cane Corso owners, proper care includes strict weight management and slow growth from puppyhood through maturity. Our growth timeline guide explains how to adjust feeding as your dog approaches skeletal maturity. Consistent weight checks are key; use our Cane Corso weight chart as a reference.

Exercise Timing and Type

High-impact exercise on hard surfaces before the growth plates close (around 12 to 16 months) can contribute to microdamage in the developing joint. A prospective study on giant breeds found that puppies exposed to more than 30 minutes of forced running daily before 12 months had higher PennHIP distraction indices. Owners should stick to controlled, low-impact activities like swimming or short leash walks until the dog is fully grown, as recommended in our care guide. Pushing a young Cane Corso too hard can have lifelong consequences.

Owner Decisions That Reduce Hip Dysplasia Risk

Selecting a Puppy With Screened Parents

The single most important decision an owner can make is to purchase a Cane Corso puppy from a breeder who provides OFA or PennHIP scores for both parents. The AKC marketplace lists breeders, but the buyer must verify health testing independently. A breeder who refuses to share OFA scores should be avoided entirely. For more on breeder evaluation, see our about page on standards. This step is non-negotiable for preventing the condition from perpetuating.

Maintaining Lean Body Condition

Keeping your Cane Corso at a lean body condition score (BCS 4 to 5 out of 9) throughout life reduces the mechanical load on the hips. Studies in giant breed populations demonstrate that dogs maintained at ideal weight develop osteoarthritis 2 to 3 years later than overweight dogs, even when dysplasia is present. Owners should weigh their dog monthly and adjust food accordingly. A lean dog is a healthier dog, especially for a breed predisposed to joint issues.

Supplements and Veterinary Care

While no supplement can prevent hip dysplasia, omega-3 fatty acids and glucosamine-chondroitin products may improve joint comfort in dogs with existing dysplasia. Clinical trials in large breeds show a modest benefit for dogs with mild to moderate osteoarthritis. If your dog shows persistent lameness, consult a veterinarian immediately. Red-flag symptoms include sudden non-weight-bearing lameness, crying when touched, or inability to stand. These require urgent vet care. Never rely solely on supplements to manage severe pain.

Prognosis and Long-Term Management for Affected Dogs

Non-Surgical Management

For dogs with mild to moderate dysplasia, conservative management includes weight control, physical therapy, joint supplements, and NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs) as needed. Many Cane Corso with Fair OFA ratings live comfortable lives with this approach. Outcome studies show that 65% of dogs treated conservatively maintain acceptable mobility for 4 to 6 years after diagnosis. Regular veterinary check-ups are essential to adjust the management plan as the dog ages.

Surgical Options

When conservative management fails, surgery may be indicated. Options include femoral head ostectomy (FHO) or total hip replacement (THR). THR has excellent outcomes in giant breeds, with 90% to 95% of dogs returning to near-normal function. Long-term follow-up in giant breeds confirms THR as the gold standard for severe dysplasia in Cane Corso. However, surgery is costly ($3,500 to $7,000 per hip) and requires a specialist. FAQs About Cane Corso Hip Dysplasia Rate Statistics

What is the exact Cane Corso hip dysplasia rate according to OFA data?

The OFA database reports a 19.8% dysplastic rate for Cane Corso submitted for final certification. However, nearly 50% receive Fair or Dysplastic ratings overall. Source OFA.

Can hip dysplasia be prevented entirely in Cane Corso?

Because the condition is hereditary, it cannot be entirely prevented, but careful selection of parents with Excellent or Good OFA scores reduces risk dramatically. Environmental factors such as weight control also play a role. Source.

At what age should I have my Cane Corso screened for hip dysplasia?

PennHIP can be done at 16 weeks of age for early detection. OFA preliminary evaluation is possible at 12 months, with final certification at 24 months. Source OFA.

What does a "Fair" OFA rating mean for my Cane Corso?

A Fair rating indicates borderline joint conformation. It is not dysplastic, but the dog has higher risk of developing osteoarthritis later in life compared to dogs with Good or Excellent ratings. Source.

Is hip dysplasia more common in male or female Cane Corso?

Data from the OFA does not show a statistically significant difference between sexes in Cane Corso. Both sexes are affected at similar rates.

Can a Cane Corso with hip dysplasia still live a normal life?

Yes, many dogs with mild to moderate dysplasia adapt well with weight management, supplements, and low-impact exercise. Severe cases may require surgery. Source.

How does rapid growth contribute to hip dysplasia in Cane Corso puppies?

Excessive caloric intake and rapid growth place mechanical stress on developing joints. Studies show that overfed puppies have higher rates of joint laxity. Source.

Should I buy from a breeder who does not screen for hip dysplasia?

No. Responsible breeders always screen. Buyers should request OFA or PennHIP scores on both parents. The AKC breed page provides guidance, but does not require certification.

What are the first signs of hip dysplasia in a Cane Corso puppy?

Bunny-hopping gait, difficulty getting up after lying down, and reluctance to jump or climb stairs. These signs warrant a veterinary examination.

How does hip dysplasia affect Cane Corso life expectancy?

Hip dysplasia itself does not shorten lifespan, but it can lead to obesity and reduced exercise, which may affect overall health. With proper management, a Cane Corso with dysplasia can live a full lifespan (9 to 10 years).

Can I get a refund if my puppy develops hip dysplasia?

Some breeders offer health guarantees that cover hip dysplasia up to a certain age. Always review the contract before purchase. For more, see our privacy policy regarding purchase agreements.

Is PennHIP better than OFA for Cane Corso?

PennHIP is more sensitive for early detection and can be done earlier (16 weeks vs 12 months). OFA is the standard for breeding certification at 24 months. Both have value.

Does neutering affect hip dysplasia risk in Cane Corso?

Some studies in large breeds suggest that early neutering (before 12 months) may increase risk of orthopedic disease. Consult your veterinarian for breed-specific advice.

Where can I find OFA test results for a specific Cane Corso?

Visit the OFA website and search the database by dog's name, AKC number, or breeder's name.

What is the success rate of total hip replacement in Cane Corso?

Total hip replacement has a 90% to 95% success rate for restoring normal function in giant breeds. Complications are rare with experienced surgeons. Source.
